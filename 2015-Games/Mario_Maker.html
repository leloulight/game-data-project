Mario Maker is an upcoming side-scroller game creation game, developed and published by Nintendo for the Wii U video game console, scheduled for release in September 2015. The game is based upon a level editor, in which users can create and play their own Mario levels.

During its E3 2014 unveiling, it was depicted that players can easily switch between two different visual styles: the original Super Mario Bros. 8-bit NES graphics, and the New Super Mario Bros. U style of HD graphics.[4][5] They later showed that players will additionally be able to switch to and from Super Mario Bros. 3 and Super Mario World graphics during the 2014 Video Game Awards. Nintendo plans to include more visual styles, even from non-Mario games.[6]

Development[edit]

The game was conceived as a tool by Nintendo's internal development team, to be used within the company. The team, however, quickly realized the tool's potential as a game and pitched the idea to senior game designer Takashi Tezuka.[7]

Meanwhile, Tezuka had been wanting to make a Wii U followup to Mario Paint that uses the Wii U GamePad.[7] Upon seeing the Mario Maker tool however, Tezuka realized that a level making tool was more marketable than a mere drawing program. He noted to Polygon that building levels is "not as difficult or out of reach as drawing is" but that he "was inspired to bring the fun of Mario Paint into this course editor".[7] The game is being directed by Yosuke Oshino, who previously worked as a programmer on Pikmin, Pikmin 2, and New Super Mario Bros. Wii.[8]

Controversy[edit]

Mario Maker has often been a source of speculation as the basis for Nintendo's actions in early 2015 removing videos of Super Mario World ROM hacks as well as tool-assisted speedruns of their games on Niconico video.[9][10] It is likely that in order to remove visible competition to Mario Maker, several prominent Niconico users were targeted, resulting in their ROM hack videos, at times featuring over 300,000 views, being deleted.[11] This is likely in part a follow-up to their recent, controversial Nintendo Partnership Program.
