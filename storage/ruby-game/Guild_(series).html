Guild (series)

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
Jump to: navigation, search

The Guild series (ギルドシリーズ, Girudo Shirīzu?) is a compilation of video games produced by Level-5 for the Nintendo 3DS in cooperation with various game designers. The first compilation, Guild01 (ギルド01, Girudo Zero Wan?),[1][2] consists of four games and was released at retail in Japan on May 31, 2012.[3][4] Three of the titles have been announced for individual release on the Nintendo eShop in Western territories during Fall 2012.[5] All four of them were released individually on the Japanese eShop not long after. A second compilation, Guild02 (ギルド02, Girudo Zero Tsū?), which features three games designed by Keiji Inafune, Kazuya Asano, Takemaru Abiko and Kaz Ayabe, was released on the Nintendo eShop in Japan during March 2013 and began release in Western territories in May 2013.[6][7]

Guild01 was released as a compilation for Nintendo 3DS in Japan on May 31, 2012 and consists of four completely distinct games, designed by Goichi Suda, Yoot Saito, Yasumi Matsuno and Yoshiyuki Hirai of comedy duo America Zarigani.[10] The game was bundled with a playable demo of Level-5's game, Time Travelers.[11] They were later released on the Nintendo eShop in late 2012, with the exception of 'Weapon Shop de Omasse,' which was released in February 2014.

Liberation Maiden[edit]

Liberation Maiden (解放少女, Kaihō Shōjo?) is Goichi Suda's contribution to Guild01. In this shooter game, an invading country is stealing all of Japan's energy. The player controls Shoko Ōzora (voiced by Kana Hanazawa),[12] a high school girl who became President of New Japan after her father's assassination, as she enters the mech Kamui to fight back.[3] It features animated cutscenes created by Studio BONES and the main theme song, "Day of Liberation" (解放の日, Kaihō no Hi?), performed by Marina.[13] The game was released in Europe on October 4, 2012, in North America on October 25, 2012 and individually in Japan on November 14, 2012. The game was later released internationally on iOS on March 7, 2013.[14]

A visual novel sequel to the game, titled Liberation Maiden SIN, was developed by 5pb. for PlayStation 3 and was released in Japan on December 5, 2013.[15][16] A PlayStation Vita version was released on July 31, 2014.[17]

Aero Porter[edit]

Aero Porter (エアロポーター, Earo Pōtā?) is a simulation game directed by Yoot Saito. The player is in charge of an airport, and their goal is to manage baggage quickly and ensure that the airplanes are able to depart on time. If the player is successful, their airport's rating will grow, gaining them more money and passengers as well as allowing them to purchase new airplanes and expand their airport.[18] The game was released in Europe and North America on November 29, 2012 and individually in Japan on December 5, 2012.

Crimson Shroud[edit]

Crimson Shroud (クリムゾンシュラウド, Kurimuzon Shuraudo?) is a traditional adventure RPG created by Yasumi Matsuno, developed internally at Level-5[12] alongside Nex Entertainment.[8] The player is a "Chaser", someone who is an expert at searching for people. The game is set 1,000 years in the past in a world without magic, and the story details how magic became commonplace in its world.[18] The game was released on the Nintendo eShop in Japan on November 28, 2012 and in Europe and North America on December 13, 2012. The game features music by Hitoshi Sakimoto's company, Basiscape, with music from him, Mitsuhiro Kaneda, Azusa Chiba, Kimihiro Abe, and Masaharu Iwata.[19]

Weapon Shop de Omasse[edit]

Weapon Shop de Omasse (レンタル武器屋 de オマッセ, Rentaru Bukiya de Omasse?, lit. Omasse's Rental Weapon Shop) is a fantasy rhythm RPG written by Yoshiyuki Hirai, in which the main characters are a father and son pair who craft weapons for heroes. The player uses rhythmic gameplay to forge weapons which are rented by heroes for their quests; the weapon chosen will affect whether or not the hero is successful.[18] The game was released individually on the Japanese Nintendo eShop on November 21, 2012. Originally, this was the only title not to be announced for Western release, with the publisher citing the high degree of translation required and debatable audience size for the game for a localization to be produced.[5][20] In January 2014, a classification of the game was posted by the Australian Classification Board under the title, Weapon Shop de Omasse, suggesting that the game would receive a localization.[21] The game was released on February 20, 2014 after the announcement during Nintendo Direct on February 13, 2014.

A follow up title, titled Guild02, was announced on May 24, 2012. The compilation features three games designed by Keiji Inafune, Kazuya Asano, Takemaru Abiko and Kaz Ayabe.[22] The compilation has not been announced for a retail release. These games were released on the Nintendo eShop in Japan in March 2013, and in North America and Europe between May and July 2013.[23]

Attack of the Friday Monsters![edit]

Attack of the Friday Monsters! (怪獣が出る金曜日, Kaijū ga Deru Kinyōbi?, lit. Monster Come Out on Fridays) is a life simulation game designed by Kaz Ayabe, who is known in Japan for the popular Boku no Natsuyasumi series. The game follows a boy named Sohta who lives in a town where monsters and superheroes from 1970s tokusatsu shows appear every Friday. The game was released in Japan on March 13, 2013 and in North America and Europe on July 18, 2013.

Bugs vs. Tanks[edit]

Bugs vs. Tanks (虫けら戦車, Mushikera Sensha?) is a tank action game designed by Keiji Inafune. The game sees players take the role of various World War II tanks piloted by Nazi soldiers that have been shrunk down to miniature size in order to fight against various bugs and insects. The game was released in Japan on March 19, 2013 and in North America and Europe on June 20, 2013.

The Starship Damrey[edit]

The Starship Damrey (宇宙船ダムレイ号, Uchūsen Damurei-gō?) is a first-person perspective survival horror game designed by Kazuya Asano and Takemaru Abiko. The game throws players into a suspenseful environment with no tutorials or hints to guide them. The game was released in Japan on March 27, 2013 and in North America and Europe on May 16, 2013.[24]

Reception[edit]

Famitsu gave Guild01 a score of 34/40.[25] In an import review, ComputerAndVideoGames.com gave the game a score of 7.5, saying that whilst the individual games have a lot of charm, the differences between them are too great for them to work as a single package.[26]
