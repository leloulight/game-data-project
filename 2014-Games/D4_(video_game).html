D4: Dark Dreams Don't Die is an episodic video game developed by Access Games and published by Microsoft Studios exclusively for the Xbox One.[3] The game was announced during Microsoft's E3 2013 press event.[3] The title represents the phrase "Dark Dreams Don't Die," as well as the fourth dimension (time).[4] The game is unrelated to the D video game series.[4] D4 was released one day early on September 18, 2014.[5]

Gameplay[edit]

The main character is David Young, a private investigator whose wife has been murdered.[4] The traumatic events surrounding her death left Young unable to recollect those memories, but has allowed him to travel through time.[4] Young travels through time to undo the murder.[4] The player cannot time travel arbitrarily, as the powers are activated upon finding certain objects that bring the player to specific points in time.[4]

D4 is an Xbox One title that uses Kinect controls with "simple gestures and voice" to uncover the mystery.[4] The game uses cel-shaded graphics similar to that of a graphic novel.[4] Hidetaka Suehiro is the game's director.[4]

D4 was nominated for "Destructoid's Best of E3 2014" as "Best Xbox Exclusive" at E3 in 2014.[26] It was also nominated for "TGS Awards 2014" in the category XBox at TGS, and for "4Gamer Awards" as "Rookie Exclusive" on October 1, 2014.[27]

D4 was met with positive reviews from critics, with praise directed towards the game's depth, attention to detail, and bizarreness, with many drawing comparison to Deadly Premonition. It received an average score of 77% from GameRankings and 77/100 from Metacritic.[7]

The Game Scouts[16] gave D4 a 9/10, stating: "D4: Dark Dreams Don't Die is one of the most memorable games in recent memory. It may not be for everyone, but gamers with an open mind and Swery65's longtime devotees are going to treasure this exceptional journey."

Daniel Weissenberger of GameCritics wrote a more mixed review of the game, in particular regard to the game's length, stating, "I wasn't expecting a complete game from D4 - the episodic model guarantees a certain amount of waiting around on the part of the consumer - but this doesn't even feel like a complete episode," while still praising the game's detective and investigation aspects, ultimately giving the game a 6.5/10.[17]
